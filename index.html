🫁 The Pathway of Air

1Nostrils (Nares)
External openings lined with coarse hairs (vibrissae) that trap dust, pollen, and large particles before air enters deeper structures.
2Nasal Cavity
Scroll-like turbinate bones create turbulence that warms air to body temperature, adds moisture, and traps fine particles in sticky mucus swept by cilia.
3Pharynx (Throat)
A 13 cm muscular funnel shared by air and food. The nasopharynx, oropharynx, and laryngopharynx direct air downward while the epiglottis stands guard.
4Larynx (Voice Box)
Houses the vocal folds and the epiglottis — a leaf-shaped flap that snaps shut during swallowing, preventing food from entering the airway.
5Trachea (Windpipe)
A 10–12 cm tube held open by 16–20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings. Ciliated cells sweep mucus and trapped particles upward toward the throat.
6Primary Bronchi
At the carina, the trachea forks into the right bronchus (wider, more vertical — so foreign objects often land here) and the left bronchus.
7Bronchioles
Branching passages under 1 mm wide with no cartilage. Smooth muscle in their walls contracts or relaxes to control airflow — key in asthma.
8Lungs
Spongy, cone-shaped organs filling the thoracic cavity. The right lung has 3 lobes; the left has 2 (to accommodate the heart). Together they hold ~6 litres of air.
9Alveoli
~300 million microscopic air sacs per lung, total surface area of a tennis court. Walls just one cell thick allow O₂ to diffuse into blood and CO₂ to diffuse out.
💡 Did you know?

The respiratory system processes about 11,000 litres of air every single day.

🔬 Alveolar Gas Exchange
O₂

Diffuses from alveolus → capillary blood (high→low concentration)

CO₂

Diffuses from capillary blood → alveolus (waste removal)
